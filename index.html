Justin Chenjia Zhang

Hi! I am Justin, an incoming master's student in Computer Science at the University of Chicago. I received a B.S. in Computer Science with honors (Machine Learning Specialization) and a B.S. in Mathematics from the University of Chicago in June 2026. During my undergraduate studies, I was fortunate to work with Prof. Sarah Sebo and Prof. Nancy Kawalek.

Through my undergraduate research, I discovered a passion for human-computer interaction and human-robot interaction, especially designing socially intelligent robots that can build positive relationships with people and support human well-being.

Research

Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) Lab

Jun. 2024 – Present

[Honors Thesis] User Perceptions of Agency in LLM-Controlled Social Robots

  • Conducting a between-subjects study on user perceptions of LLM-controlled social robots; preliminary results suggest that users perceive LLM-controlled robots more similarly to autonomous robots than to teleoperated robots

[HRI 2026] Can You Help Me? The Influence of Robot Requests for Help on Child-Robot Connection

  • Contributed to study design, including task flow, experimental conditions, and behavioral measures
  • Led autonomous robot system development for emotional expression and collaborative gameplay on a Misty II robot, including algorithm design, speech interaction pipeline, TCP-based gameplay integration, and prompt engineering
  • Conducted a between-subjects study (N=100) with 6–10-year-old children, showing that a robot’s asking for help builds trust between a robot and a child, and that expressing emotional vulnerability creates further feelings of connection

T. Flanagan, J. C. Zhang, L. Bian, & S. Sebo

[HRI 2025] Exploring Robot Personality Traits and Their Influence on User Affect and Experience

  • Developed a method using large language models to generate dialogue consistent with different personality traits, providing a scalable and flexible approach for future robot personality research
  • Programmed a teleoperated NAO robot with three distinct personalities (expected, high extraversion, high neuroticism)
  • Conducted a between-subjects study (N=66), showing that both high extraversion and high neuroticism personalities significantly enhanced participants’ emotional states, with high extraversion rated most enjoyable

W. Zhang, C. Kovacs, L. Pablo, J. Zhang, M. Bai, S. Jeong, & S. Sebo

Scientists, Technologists, and Artists Generating Exploration (STAGE) Center

Nov. 2023 – Jun. 2025

Exploring the Quantum Labyrinth: Teaching Physics Through Games

  • Led an interdisciplinary student team developing and evaluating Quantum Labyrinth, a digital game designed to make quantum physics accessible to non-experts
  • Designed and conducted two user evaluations with UChicago faculty, students, and children to collect feedback on concept clarity, engagement, and learning outcomes, integrating findings into iterative game improvements
  • Led weekly two-hour sessions for five new team members on GameMaker Language and embodied physics concepts
  • Quantum Labyrinth was presented at the 2025 American Physical Society Joint March Meeting & April Meeting, and we shared our lessons learned at the 2025 UChicago Undergraduate Research Symposium.
